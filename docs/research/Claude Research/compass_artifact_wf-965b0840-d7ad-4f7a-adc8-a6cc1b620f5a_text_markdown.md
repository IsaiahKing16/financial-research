# Matrix profiles for financial time series: powerful descriptive tool, unproven signal generator

**Matrix profiles provide an elegant, near-parameter-free framework for exhaustive subsequence similarity search — but no published study demonstrates they generate actionable trading signals.** The STUMPY library implements O(n²) exact nearest-neighbor search across all subsequences of a time series, enabling motif discovery, anomaly detection, and regime segmentation as natural by-products. For a universe of 500 daily equities with 10 years of history, self-joins complete in roughly 15 seconds and all-pairs cross-joins in under 90 minutes on modest hardware. The critical gap is not computational — it is empirical. Only two peer-reviewed papers apply matrix profiles to equity markets, both descriptive, and the method's z-normalization discards the magnitude information that often drives financial decisions. Whether matrix profiles add value beyond KNN on engineered features depends entirely on whether raw shape similarity in price subsequences carries predictive content — a hypothesis no one has rigorously tested.

## What matrix profiles compute and why the math matters

A **matrix profile** is a vector P of length (n − m + 1) that stores, for every contiguous subsequence of length m in a time series of length n, the z-normalized Euclidean distance to its nearest non-trivial neighbor. The companion **matrix profile index** I stores which position that nearest neighbor lives at. Together, they compress an O(n²) all-pairs distance matrix into O(n) space while preserving the single most important relationship — the best match.

The foundational operation is the **distance profile**: given a query subsequence T_{i,m} = [t_i, t_{i+1}, ..., t_{i+m−1}], compute its z-normalized Euclidean distance to every other subsequence in the series. Z-normalization subtracts the local mean and divides by the local standard deviation of each window, making comparisons **amplitude- and offset-invariant** — only shape matters. The distance satisfies d² = 2m(1 − ρ), where ρ is the Pearson correlation between the two raw subsequences, so minimizing distance is equivalent to maximizing correlation.

The word "subsequence" here means something precise and different from a feature vector. A subsequence is literally m consecutive raw values sliced from the time series — if you apply it to closing prices, it is m consecutive closing prices. It is not a vector of returns, volatility measures, or momentum indicators computed from OHLCV bars. This distinction is fundamental: **matrix profiles operate on raw temporal shape; KNN analogue matching operates on domain-engineered statistical summaries**.

**STOMP** (Scalable Time series Ordered-search Matrix Profile), introduced by Zhu et al. at IEEE ICDM 2016, achieves O(n²) time complexity by exploiting the QT trick — the dot product between consecutive subsequence pairs can be incrementally updated as QT_{i,j} = QT_{i−1,j−1} − t_{i−1}·t_{j−1} + t_{i+m−1}·t_{j+m−1}, replacing O(n log n) FFT computations with O(n) arithmetic per row. The earlier **STAMP** algorithm uses MASS (FFT-based distance profile computation) at O(n log n) per profile, yielding O(n² log n) total but offering an "anytime" property — random-order processing gives high-quality approximations at any interruption point. Critically, the subsequence length m enters only the constant factor, not the big-O, allowing m up to 100,000 without complexity degradation.

STUMPY implements all variants: `stumpy.stump()` for Numba-parallelized STOMP, `stumpy.gpu_stump()` for CUDA acceleration, `stumpy.stumped()` for Dask/Ray distributed computation, and `stumpy.stumpi()` for streaming updates at O(n) per new point. The library returns a four-column array: matrix profile distances, indices, left indices, and right indices — the left/right decomposition enables causal analysis where only past (or future) matches are considered.

## Self-join finds recurring patterns; AB-join finds cross-asset echoes

The **self-join** computes the matrix profile of a time series against itself, answering: for every subsequence in this series, where does the most similar shape occur elsewhere in the same series? An **exclusion zone** of ±⌈m/4⌉ around each query position prevents trivial matches with overlapping subsequences. The global minimum of the self-join matrix profile locates the **top motif** (most repeated pattern); the global maximum locates the **top discord** (most anomalous subsequence).

The **AB-join** computes the matrix profile between two different time series T_A and T_B: for every subsequence in T_A, find its nearest neighbor in T_B. No exclusion zone is needed because the series are independent. A critical property: **P_{AB} ≠ P_{BA}** — the join is asymmetric. Finding that patterns in AAPL match patterns in MSFT does not mean the reverse mapping is identical.

For cross-ticker pattern matching, the AB-join has direct applications. Low values in the AB-join matrix profile indicate where ticker A's price shape closely mirrors ticker B's — identifying shared reactions to macro events, sector co-movement, or potential hedging relationships. High values (discords) reveal where A's behavior has no analog in B, potentially flagging idiosyncratic moves. To detect lead-lag relationships, one could compute the AB-join of A's subsequences against time-shifted versions of B, looking for systematic offsets where A's patterns precede B's by a fixed lag.

Computationally, self-joins exploit the distance matrix's symmetry (only the upper triangle is needed), while AB-joins must compute the full rectangular matrix of dimensions (n_A − m + 1) × (n_B − m + 1), giving O(n_A · n_B) complexity. In STUMPY, the AB-join is invoked as `stumpy.stump(T_A, m, T_B=T_B, ignore_trivial=False)`, and GPU and distributed variants support it identically.

## FLUSS detects regime boundaries without parametric assumptions

The **FLUSS** algorithm (Fast Low-cost Unipotent Semantic Segmentation), formalized by Gharghabi et al. in *Data Mining and Knowledge Discovery* (2019), derives regime boundaries exclusively from the matrix profile index — not the distance values. The intuition is geometric: if a time series contains distinct regimes, subsequences within each regime tend to match other subsequences in the same regime, creating a pattern of nearest-neighbor connections that rarely cross regime boundaries.

The algorithm constructs an **arc curve** by drawing a conceptual arc from each position i to its nearest-neighbor index I_i, then counting how many arcs cross each time point t. This is implemented efficiently via a prefix-sum technique in O(n). The raw arc curve is biased — fewer arcs naturally cross near the series endpoints — so FLUSS divides by an **idealized arc curve** (a parabola modeling random arc placement) to produce the **corrected arc curve (CAC)**, bounded in [0, 1]. Local minima in the CAC indicate regime boundaries: **a CAC value near zero means almost no nearest-neighbor connections cross that time point**, strongly suggesting a behavioral change.

Regime boundaries are extracted greedily: find the global CAC minimum, apply an exclusion zone of 5× the subsequence length, repeat for the desired number of regimes. STUMPY implements this as `stumpy.fluss(mp_index, L, n_regimes)` and provides a streaming variant `stumpy.floss()` for online regime detection.

Compared to **HMM-based regime detection**, FLUSS offers three advantages: no distributional assumptions (financial returns violate Gaussian emission models), no need to pre-specify the number of hidden states (though n_regimes must be chosen for boundary extraction), and deterministic computation versus EM's sensitivity to initialization. HMMs counter with transition probability estimates directly useful for risk management, native multivariate support, probabilistic regime membership at each time point, and forward-looking regime forecasts — capabilities FLUSS entirely lacks. FLUSS identifies *where* regimes change but provides no characterization of *what* changed; a separate post-hoc analysis is needed to label each segment.

Against **threshold-based detection** (e.g., VIX > 25 defines a high-volatility regime), FLUSS is data-driven and can discover boundaries invisible to any single indicator. But threshold methods are faster, fully interpretable, and work reliably when the relevant indicator is known. For financial applications, **FLUSS is best positioned as a complementary exploratory tool** — confirming or discovering regime boundaries that parametric models or threshold rules might miss, particularly when applied to transformed data like rolling volatility or return autocorrelations rather than raw prices.

## Computational reality: daily equities are a trivial workload

STOMP's O(n²) complexity sounds imposing, but daily financial data is short by matrix profile standards. STUMPY's official benchmarks on dual-Xeon hardware show self-join times of **0.02 seconds for n = 1,024** and **0.22 seconds for n = 4,096** with m = 50. For 500 equities with 10 years of daily data (n ≈ 2,520 each), self-joins complete in roughly **0.03–0.05 seconds per ticker, or about 15 seconds total** on a dual-CPU server.

The expensive case is the all-pairs AB-join for cross-ticker analysis: **124,750 independent pairs** at ~0.04 seconds each requires approximately **83 minutes on 2 CPUs**. Because each pair is independent, this parallelizes trivially — with 16 cores, expect ~20 minutes; with a 32-worker Dask cluster distributing pairs (not using `stumped` per pair, which adds coordination overhead exceeding the computation), approximately **3 minutes**. Memory footprint is modest: each matrix profile consumes ~20 KB, so all 124,750 AB-join profiles total roughly **2.5 GB**.

GPU acceleration via `stumpy.gpu_stump()` is **counterproductive for daily financial data**. STUMPY's benchmarks reveal ~1–30 seconds of GPU kernel launch and data transfer overhead, exceeding the entire CPU computation time for n < 65,000. A single GTX 1080 Ti only outpaces 2 CPUs at n > 100,000; the DGX-2 with 16 V100s only dominates above n ≈ 500,000. For daily equities, stick to CPU. GPU becomes valuable for tick data (millions of points per series) or for processing concatenated multi-year intraday bars.

For incremental daily updates, `stumpy.stumpi()` processes each new data point in O(n) — effectively instantaneous at **~450+ points per second** on modest hardware. The practical architecture is: batch-compute matrix profiles once, then maintain them via streaming updates as each new trading day's data arrives. Scaling beyond 500 tickers follows straightforward math: 1,000 tickers yield ~500,000 pairs requiring ~5.5 hours on 2 CPUs or ~20 minutes distributed; at 5,000 tickers (12.5 million pairs), pre-clustering tickers before cross-joining becomes necessary.

For comparison, building an HNSW index over 1.25 million feature vectors (500 tickers × 2,500 days) takes roughly 1 minute, with queries at ~1 ms each — approximately **21 minutes for exhaustive nearest-neighbor retrieval** across all points. The approaches answer different questions and are better understood as complementary than competing.

## The academic evidence is thin and entirely descriptive

A systematic search for peer-reviewed applications of matrix profiles to financial markets reveals a stark finding: **only two papers directly apply matrix profiles to equity data, both from the same research group, both purely descriptive**. Cartwright, Crane, and Ruskin (2021, MDPI Engineering Proceedings; 2022, MDPI Forecasting) study the S&P 500 and Microsoft stock during the 2007–2009 financial crisis, demonstrating that matrix profile motifs successfully identify similar behavioral periods and that shorter subsequence lengths yield more numerous motifs in financial series. Neither paper backtests a trading strategy, reports risk-adjusted returns, or claims predictive power.

The broader matrix profile literature is prolific — over 28 papers in the series from the UCR/Keogh lab — and matrix profiles achieve state-of-the-art anomaly detection performance across general time series benchmarks. Yeh et al. (2024, arXiv) benchmarked multidimensional matrix profiles against 19 methods on 119 datasets, finding it "the only method that consistently delivers high performance across all setups." But these benchmarks do not include financial prediction tasks.

On the KNN comparison specifically, Mondal, Akbarinia, and Masseglia (2023, *Data Mining and Knowledge Discovery*) explicitly frame the matrix profile as a 1-nearest-neighbor method and propose extending it to k > 1 neighbors, finding improved motif and discord discovery. This clarifies that **matrix profile versus KNN is partially a false dichotomy — the matrix profile is a specialized, highly efficient implementation of 1NN subsequence search**. The real distinction is what space the nearest-neighbor search operates in: raw z-normalized subsequences versus engineered feature vectors.

Guidotti and D'Onofrio (2021, *Frontiers in AI*) compared their matrix-profile-based classifier (MAPIC) against kNN-DTW on the UCR archive, finding competitive accuracy with superior interpretability. However, this is a classification benchmark, not a financial prediction study.

## The critical assessment: shape matching versus regime characterization

The question of whether matrix profiles add value beyond KNN on normalized feature vectors reduces to whether **raw temporal shape similarity carries information that engineered features miss**. Several considerations favor skepticism for financial applications.

First, z-normalization discards magnitude. A 10% crash and a 1% dip with identical shapes match perfectly. In financial markets, magnitude is often the most important feature — a slow grind higher and a sharp spike both produce "up" patterns, but their implications for mean reversion, volatility, and liquidity are radically different. Feature-based KNN naturally encodes magnitude through engineered features like return magnitude, realized volatility, and drawdown depth.

Second, matrix profiles are **fundamentally univariate**. MSTUMP extends to multivariate data but at O(d × n²) cost and with limited AB-join support. Financial regime identification inherently requires multivariate context — returns, volatility, correlations, credit spreads, and order flow interact to define market states. KNN on feature vectors handles **arbitrary dimensionality** with no structural penalty, naturally incorporating OHLCV data, cross-asset features, and derived signals.

Third, the exhaustive nature of matrix profiles — computing nearest neighbors for every subsequence position — is a strength for exploratory pattern discovery but a weakness for prediction. The resulting motifs and discords are descriptive: they tell you "this pattern occurred before at time j" but not whether that recurrence has forward-looking implications. **No mechanism within the matrix profile framework connects historical pattern similarity to future return predictability.** KNN analogue matching, by contrast, is explicitly constructed for prediction — the feature space is designed so that similar feature vectors have historically preceded similar outcomes.

Fourth, the matrix profile's single parameter (window size m) is both a strength and a limitation. It eliminates feature-engineering choices but forces a fixed temporal resolution. Financial patterns manifest at multiple timescales simultaneously; a 20-day window captures swing-trading patterns but misses intraday microstructure or multi-month macro cycles. The Pan-Matrix Profile (varying m) helps but dramatically increases computation.

Where matrix profiles likely do add unique value is in three specific use cases:

- **Discord discovery for anomaly detection**: identifying subsequences with no historical precedent is genuinely useful for risk monitoring — it's a different question than feature-space outlier detection, because it captures temporal structure that point-wise features may miss
- **AB-join for lead-lag discovery**: the exhaustive cross-series comparison can reveal temporal relationships between assets that feature-based approaches might not encode, particularly if the relationship is shape-based rather than return-based  
- **FLUSS for unsupervised regime boundary identification**: as a complementary, assumption-free check on parametric regime models, FLUSS can confirm or discover boundaries that HMMs or threshold methods miss

## Conclusion

Matrix profiles represent rigorous, well-engineered machinery for exhaustive subsequence similarity search, backed by solid computer science and efficient implementations. STUMPY makes the algorithms accessible and handles financial-scale daily data with ease — **computation is not the bottleneck**. The bottleneck is the absence of evidence that the specific type of similarity matrix profiles capture (z-normalized shape of raw price subsequences) translates into tradeable signals.

For practitioners already using KNN on engineered features, the matrix profile's marginal contribution is likely confined to three niches: anomaly surveillance via discord discovery, exploratory cross-asset lead-lag analysis via AB-join, and assumption-free regime boundary detection via FLUSS. These are valuable analytical capabilities, but they complement rather than replace feature-based nearest-neighbor methods. The matrix profile answers "when did this shape occur before?" — a descriptive question. Feature-based KNN answers "what happened next when conditions were similar?" — a predictive question. Both questions matter, but only the second directly generates trading signals, and the published literature has not bridged this gap for matrix profiles.
