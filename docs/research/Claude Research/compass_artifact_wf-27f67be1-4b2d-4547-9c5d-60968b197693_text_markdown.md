# Synthetic data will likely poison your analogue engine

**For a KNN-based system like FPPE that derives its edge from finding real historical analogues, the evidence overwhelmingly argues against augmenting the analogue database with synthetic samples.** Across multiple independent research lines — from calibration studies showing SMOTE degrades Brier scores, to HNSW graph theory suggesting synthetic nodes displace real neighbors, to the fundamental epistemological problem that synthetic analogues are fabricated precedents — the risks dominate the benefits. The strongest evidence for augmentation helping financial ML comes from Fons et al. (2020), who showed up to **400% improvement in risk-adjusted returns** on small datasets using basic transformations, but their system was a classification model, not a nearest-neighbor retrieval system. The more productive path for FPPE is feature-space enrichment and regime-conditional sample weighting — approaches that improve neighbor quality without contaminating the search space with non-real patterns.

---

## Time-series augmentation methods split into "safe" and "destructive" categories

Iwana and Uchida (2021) provide the most systematic empirical evaluation, testing 12 augmentation methods across 128 UCR Archive datasets with six neural network architectures. Their taxonomy divides methods into transformation-based (magnitude domain, time domain), pattern mixing (SPAWNER, wDBA, guided warping), generative models, and decomposition methods. **Window slicing and window warping emerged as recommended first-choice methods** due to strong performance with negligible computation time. Jittering, scaling, and magnitude warping showed similar moderate benefits. Permutation was "severely detrimental" because it destroys temporal ordering entirely, while rotation (flipping) decreased accuracy for every model tested.

The critical caveat is that the UCR Archive contains **zero financial datasets** — all findings derive from sensor, ECG, motion, and spectro data. The only direct evidence on financial time series comes from Fons, Dawson, Zeng, Keane, and Iosifidis (2020), who tested augmentation methods on S&P 500 constituent returns from 1990–2019 using LSTM and TLo-NBoF models. Their results diverge meaningfully from the generic benchmarks. **Time warping performed poorly on UCR datasets but was a consistent top performer on stock data**, likely because financial patterns (momentum, mean-reversion) naturally occur at varying speeds. Their "Magnify" variant of window slicing was the strongest method, while Reverse (time reversal) degraded performance — expected for trend-following strategies. The finding that classification accuracy improvements were "very small" while Information Ratio improvements reached 400% suggests augmentation's benefit in finance operates through regularization rather than label-preserving data expansion.

For temporal structure preservation, the methods form a clear hierarchy. Jittering and scaling **fully preserve** autocorrelation, temporal ordering, and volatility clustering structure, since they only modify magnitudes while leaving timing intact. Window slicing preserves structure within the cropped window. Time warping preserves ordering but distorts timing of events. Permutation **completely destroys** all temporal dependencies — autocorrelation, volatility clustering, regime structure. Rotation preserves temporal structure but annihilates economic meaning, turning bullish patterns bearish. For financial data specifically, where volatility clustering (GARCH effects), leverage effects, and regime structure carry predictive information, only magnitude-domain transformations and window methods are defensible.

Aboussalah, Kwon, Patel, Chi, and Lee (ICLR 2023) introduced the Recursive Interpolation Method (RIM), which frames financial time series as single realizations of underlying dynamical systems and generates alternative realizations while explicitly preserving dynamics. This represents the most theoretically principled approach to financial augmentation, though its evidence base is still limited.

## GANs can reproduce some stylized facts but fail under backtesting stress

TimeGAN (Yoon, Jarrett, and van der Schaar, NeurIPS 2019) introduced a four-component architecture combining an autoencoder with adversarial training and a supervised loss for temporal coherence. It proposed the discriminative score and predictive score as evaluation metrics — the former measuring how easily a classifier can distinguish real from synthetic sequences, the latter measuring whether models trained on synthetic data can predict real data. On Google stock data, TimeGAN achieved a discriminative score of **0.102**, outperforming the next-best RCGAN at 0.196. However, TimeGAN was **never evaluated on stylized facts** — its financial test focused purely on temporal coherence metrics, not on whether generated data exhibits fat tails, volatility clustering, or leverage effects.

QuantGAN (Wiese, Knobloch, Korn, and Kretschmer, 2020, *Quantitative Finance*) was purpose-built for financial data using Temporal Convolutional Networks instead of recurrent architectures, enabling efficient capture of long-range dependencies. Tested on S&P 500 data (2009–2018), QuantGAN demonstrated reproduction of fat tails, volatility clustering, leverage effects, and serial autocorrelations "in excellent agreement" with empirical properties. The subsequent Regime-Specific QuantGAN extension (RSQGAN, 2023) revealed a critical limitation: the **same architecture failed to generalize across stocks** — a model trained on BHP could not produce realistic paths for the highly correlated stock RIO.

SigWGAN (Ni, Szpruch, Wiese, Liao, and Xiao, 2020–2024) replaced neural discriminators with signature-based Wasserstein metrics from rough path theory, eliminating adversarial training instability entirely. Published in *Mathematical Finance* (2024), it represents the most mathematically rigorous approach. Tail-GAN (Cont, Cucuringu, Xu, and Zhang, *Management Science* 2025) specifically targets tail risk by using the joint elicitability of VaR and Expected Shortfall as a loss function, achieving out-of-sample VaR errors below 10% on intraday market data.

The most sobering assessment comes from SFAG (2025, arXiv:2601.12990), which demonstrated that **baseline GANs including WGAN-GP produce unstable and unrealistic outcomes under backtesting**, even when they appear to reproduce stylized facts in post-hoc analysis. The "Can GANs Learn the Stylized Facts of Financial Time Series?" paper (ICAIF 2024) systematically evaluated five generator architectures and concluded that GANs "struggle with detailed features" and in multivariate settings "tend to generate data dependent on only one variable." No existing model satisfies all stylized facts simultaneously.

Evidence for downstream ML improvement from GAN-augmented data is thin. The strongest result comes from FTS-Diffusion (ICLR 2024) — notably a *diffusion model*, not a GAN — which reduced prediction MAPE by up to **17.9%** when augmenting real stock data. For GANs specifically, Fin-GAN (Vuletić, Prenzel, and Cucuringu, *Quantitative Finance* 2024) showed improved Sharpe ratios using an economics-driven loss function, but this was primarily a forecasting tool rather than data augmentation. The field's honest summary: most papers demonstrate fidelity (synthetic data resembles real data) rather than utility (synthetic data improves ML models).

## Bootstrap methods preserve blocks perfectly but cannot generate novel patterns

The stationary bootstrap (Politis and Romano, 1994, *JASA*) uses blocks of geometrically distributed random length, producing pseudo-time series that are stationary conditional on the original data. The moving block bootstrap (Künsch, 1989; Liu and Singh, 1992) uses fixed-length overlapping blocks. The circular bootstrap (Politis and Romano, 1992) wraps the series circularly to eliminate edge effects. All three preserve temporal dependencies **exactly within blocks** — autocorrelation, volatility clustering, and local regime structure are maintained perfectly.

The fundamental limitation is that bootstrap methods **exclusively reshuffle existing blocks**. Every sub-sequence in a bootstrapped series is an exact copy of a sub-sequence from the original data. No new local dynamics are ever generated. A bootstrap drawn from 2010–2020 data can never produce a 2008-style crisis pattern or a COVID-style crash. The novelty lies only in the *sequence* of blocks, not in the blocks themselves. At block boundaries, temporal dependencies are disrupted — a calm period can be artificially juxtaposed with crisis-level volatility, creating **spurious regime transitions** that never occurred in actual markets.

For ML training augmentation specifically (not traditional confidence intervals), the literature is sparse. Bergmeir, Hyndman, and Benítez (2016, *International Journal of Forecasting*) used MBB-based augmentation with STL decomposition for bagging exponential smoothing, showing improved forecasts. Bandara et al. (2021) demonstrated MBB augmentation improving LSTM forecasting. But these applications are forecasting models, not analogue retrieval systems. The block length parameter creates an irreducible tradeoff: blocks too short destroy dependence structure; blocks too long reduce diversity and increase variance (Politis and White, 2004, *Econometric Reviews*).

## Augmentation can inject patterns that markets never produced

The risk of spurious pattern injection is particularly acute for financial data. Block bootstrap at block boundaries creates artificial regime transitions — a model trained on such data can learn these junction artifacts as predictive signals. Scaling can distort trends and seasonality, creating "unrepresentative data samples that may mislead the model." Window slicing/cropping can systematically exclude important events if patterns are always cropped in the same way, introducing artificial bias.

The manifold framework clarifies the theoretical risk. The manifold assumption (Chapelle et al., 2006) states that high-dimensional data concentrates around a low-dimensional manifold. **On-manifold augmentation** generates samples that could plausibly be real; **off-manifold augmentation** generates samples that could never occur. Paschali et al. (IPMI 2019) demonstrated that "random augmentation can result in out-of-distribution samples, which could hinder model training." AdvWavAug (2025) proved theoretically that on-manifold augmentation improves out-of-distribution generalization more than off-manifold approaches.

The label-preservation problem is uniquely severe in finance. In image classification, a rotated cat is still a cat — the label is invariant to augmentation. **Financial time series have no such invariance guarantee.** A time-warped or magnitude-scaled price pattern may correspond to a completely different market microstate with different forward return distributions. The relationship between pattern shape and subsequent outcome is not preserved under transformation. Fons et al. (2020) found this empirically: augmentation produced "very small" accuracy improvements despite large Information Ratio gains, suggesting the augmented labels were often incorrect but the regularization effect was still beneficial — a mechanism fundamentally different from label-preserving augmentation in computer vision.

Financial data compounds these risks through non-stationarity (the stationary bootstrap assumes stationarity by name), regime changes that bootstrap methods cannot anticipate, the adversarial nature of markets (patterns get arbitraged away once discovered), and extremely low signal-to-noise ratios where noise from augmentation can overwhelm faint predictive signals. López de Prado (2018, Chapter 13) explicitly warns about backtesting on synthetic data and the amplified overfitting risk.

## Synthetic data fundamentally undermines KNN analogue retrieval and destroys calibration

This is the decisive section. The evidence converges from five independent directions against augmenting FPPE's analogue database.

**Synthetic neighbors displace real ones.** When synthetic points are added to a KNN search space, they become candidates for nearest-neighbor retrieval. Synthetic points generated by interpolation (SMOTE) or generative models sit near real cluster centroids, making them disproportionately likely to appear in query results. Elreedy and Atiya (2022, *Machine Learning*) derived that SMOTE-generated patterns "do not faithfully reproduce the original minority class density" — the distribution is systematically contracted. Radovanović et al. (JMLR, 2010) demonstrated the "hubness" phenomenon: in high-dimensional spaces, certain points become disproportionately frequent nearest neighbors. Synthetic points near cluster centers become artificial hubs that crowd out genuine historical analogues.

**HNSW graph structure is permanently altered.** When synthetic points are inserted into HNSW (Malkov and Yashunin, IEEE TPAMI, 2018), they become permanent graph nodes whose connections affect navigability. Synthetic points assigned to upper layers of the hierarchical graph become mandatory waypoints for search traversal, potentially steering queries toward synthetic neighborhoods. Connection pruning during synthetic point insertion can isolate real data points. No published study examines synthetic data in HNSW indices — the approach is unprecedented, which is itself informative.

**Probability calibration is severely degraded.** This is the most robust and damning finding, replicated across thousands of experiments. A 2025 study on model behavior under synthetic training data (arXiv:2510.05133) found that **"calibration degradation precedes accuracy loss"** — Expected Calibration Error increases 2–3 generations before accuracy drops, and degradation accelerates beyond 30% synthetic content. Van den Goorbergh et al. (JAMIA, 2022) found that SMOTE produced calibration intercepts of **−4.5 or lower**, indicating severe probability overestimation. A 9,000-experiment study across 15 models and 30 datasets (arXiv:2409.19751, 2024) found SMOTE "performed significantly worse on Brier Score, indicating it produces poorly calibrated probabilities." A separate 2,000-iteration simulation study (arXiv:2404.19494, 2024) confirmed that "oversampling corrections caused a decrease in calibration slope... consistently over-estimated risk." For FPPE, where the entire value proposition is calibrated probabilistic predictions measured by Brier Skill Score and reliability diagrams, these findings are disqualifying.

**The epistemic foundation is undermined.** FPPE's claim is: "We found historical situations matching current conditions, and here's what *actually happened* next." This carries weight precisely because the analogues are real. With synthetic data: "Some matches are real episodes; some are fabricated. The outcomes of fabricated analogues are themselves fabricated." Green and Armstrong (2007) found structured analogy-based forecasting outperformed expert judgment (46% vs. 32% accuracy), but the method's power depends on analogies being real events. Shumailov et al. (Nature, 2024) showed that models trained on recursively generated synthetic data undergo "model collapse," systematically **forgetting improbable events** — precisely the rare tail episodes that are FPPE's most valuable analogues.

**The one legitimate use case doesn't apply.** Murph et al. (PLOS Computational Biology, 2025) developed the Synthetic Method of Analogues (sMOA) for epidemic forecasting, using synthetic analogues **only when no real historical analogues exist** — a novel pandemic with zero precedents. FPPE operates in the opposite regime: real historical analogues exist and are the system's primary asset.

No augmentation method identified in this survey is theoretically compatible with a system whose value derives from the realness of retrieved analogues. Feature-space augmentation (enriching real observations with additional features) is the only approach that improves neighbor quality without contaminating the search space, because every returned neighbor remains a genuine historical episode.

## Feature enrichment and sample weighting are the productive alternatives

Rather than adding synthetic samples, four alternative approaches are directly compatible with KNN analogue systems, supported by stronger evidence.

**Feature-space augmentation** is the most naturally compatible approach. Adding technical indicators, cross-asset features, regime indicators, spectral features (Leung and Zhao, 2021, using Hilbert-Huang Transform), and fractionally differentiated features (López de Prado, 2018, Chapter 5) improves the distance metric without altering the database. Every neighbor returned is still a real historical episode, but similarity measurement becomes more discriminating. Shen et al. (ICML 2022) showed theoretically that data augmentation's benefit comes from making spurious features harder to learn — feature engineering achieves this directly.

**Sample weighting and importance sampling** modify how retrieved neighbors contribute to predictions without adding synthetic points. López de Prado's sequential bootstrap (2018, Chapter 4) constructs training sets by selecting observations with minimal temporal overlap, preventing redundant samples from dominating. Regime-conditional weighting upweights analogues from market regimes similar to current conditions. Time-decay weighting naturally downweights stale observations. Pareto Smoothed Importance Sampling (Vehtari et al., JMLR) stabilizes extreme weights using generalized Pareto distribution fits. For FPPE specifically, this means keeping the analogue database pure but weighting retrieved neighbors by their relevance to the current market regime — a philosophically clean solution.

**Transfer learning across related markets** expands the effective analogue database with real data from other instruments. Ban et al. (ICONIP, 2013) demonstrated "Referential KNN" that finds analogues across correlated S&P 500 assets, improving forecasts over univariate KNN. The 2CAT model (2025) showed successful transfer from Dow Jones to Nikkei 225, Hang Seng, and emerging market indices. Lanzetta (2024, arXiv:2409.17183) provides a systematic review showing LSTM-based transfer learning achieves lowest error rates for financial predictions when source markets are selected by similarity. This approach expands the pool of *real* analogues rather than fabricating synthetic ones.

**Meta-learning offers the most promising frontier.** X-Trend (Wood et al., 2023, arXiv:2310.10500) is essentially a learned analogue system using cross-attention over financial regime context sets, achieving **18.9% Sharpe improvement** over neural forecasters in real backtests (2018–2023) and 5× Sharpe improvement on novel unseen assets in zero-shot evaluation. This represents a neural generalization of KNN analogue finding that handles non-stationarity and regime shifts more gracefully than fixed-distance nearest-neighbor search.

---

## Conclusion

The research landscape reveals a stark asymmetry. For generic neural network classifiers operating on large datasets, simple augmentation methods — particularly window slicing, magnitude warping, and time warping — can meaningfully improve financial ML performance, with Fons et al. (2020) providing the best direct evidence. GANs have advanced substantially, with QuantGAN, SigWGAN, and Tail-GAN each reproducing subsets of financial stylized facts, though SFAG's finding that even plausible-looking GAN output "collapses under backtesting" should temper enthusiasm. Diffusion models appear to be overtaking GANs for financial generation, with FTS-Diffusion (ICLR 2024) providing the strongest downstream improvement evidence.

For FPPE specifically, the conclusion is unambiguous: **do not augment the analogue database with synthetic samples.** The calibration evidence alone is decisive — multiple studies totaling thousands of experiments show synthetic data systematically degrades probability calibration, the exact metric FPPE optimizes. The HNSW structural effects, hubness-driven neighbor displacement, and epistemological hollowing of the "real analogue" claim reinforce this conclusion from independent angles. The productive investment is in three directions: enriching the feature space to improve distance computation quality, implementing regime-conditional weighting to make retrieved analogues more relevant, and potentially expanding the database with real data from related instruments via cross-asset analogue search.
