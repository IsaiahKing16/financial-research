# KNN analogue matching rarely beats modern alternatives for equity returns

**Gradient boosted trees and even logistic regression consistently match or outperform K-nearest neighbors for daily equity return prediction**, according to the weight of academic and practitioner evidence. The most important finding is not that KNN fails entirely—it has niche value in regime detection and ensembles—but that its core mechanism (distance-based pattern matching) breaks down in exactly the high-dimensional, noisy conditions that characterize modern cross-sectional equity prediction. The absence of KNN from the standard toolkit at quantitative firms like AQR and Two Sigma is itself a signal. That said, evidence is weaker than one might expect: no top-tier quantitative finance journal has published a rigorous head-to-head of KNN versus gradient boosting for equity returns, leaving practitioners to triangulate from indirect comparisons and theoretical arguments.

---

## The gold-standard benchmarks never bothered testing KNN

The single most revealing fact about KNN's standing in quantitative finance is that **Gu, Kelly, and Xiu's "Empirical Asset Pricing via Machine Learning" (Review of Financial Studies, 2020)—the definitive ML horse-race for equity returns—did not include KNN among its methods**. This paper compared OLS, elastic net, PLS, PCR, gradient boosted regression trees, random forests, and neural networks across ~30,000 US stocks from 1957–2016 using 94 firm characteristics. Trees and neural networks achieved the best out-of-sample R², traced to their ability to capture nonlinear predictor interactions. The paper's omission of KNN, by researchers at Chicago Booth and Yale affiliated with AQR Capital, reflects the community's prior that KNN is not a serious contender at this scale.

Every major replication of the Gu-Kelly-Xiu framework tells the same story. Leippold, Wang, and Zhou (Journal of Financial Economics, 2022) applied the methodology to Chinese equities. Fieberg et al. (OR Spectrum, ~2022) tested 12,000 European stocks across 16 economies and found "shallow models, such as gradient boosted decision trees, provide more consistent and reliable performance." Hanauer and Kalsbach (2023) covered emerging markets with ~1.9 billion stock-month observations. **None of these papers included KNN.** The method has been silently excluded from the frontier of empirical asset pricing research.

Papers that do include both KNN and tree-based methods come from lower-tier venues and consistently show KNN underperforming. Khan et al. (PLOS ONE, 2023) tested nine ML models on Tesla daily data and found KNN was the least accurate at **80.5%** versus Random Forest at **91.3%** and XGBoost close behind. Avci et al. (Annals of Operations Research, ~2021) compared seven methods on Istanbul Stock Exchange high-frequency data; KNN was not among the top performers. A study in the North American Journal of Economics and Finance (2022) on Chinese index direction forecasting found XGBoost with sentiment features achieved the best accuracy, outperforming KNN, SVM, and neural networks. Singh (arXiv:2202.09359, 2022) reported that KNN's performance on Nifty 50 data specifically degraded with increasing dataset size—a finding consistent with the curse of dimensionality.

## Transformers promise more than they deliver for return prediction

The Temporal Fusion Transformer, introduced by Bryan Lim, Sercan Arık, Nicolas Loeff, and Tomas Pfister (International Journal of Forecasting, 2021), was tested on only one financial dataset: **S&P 500 realized volatility, not returns**. Its attention mechanism proved valuable for regime identification around the 2008 crisis, but the paper's financial contribution is limited to volatility forecasting, where autocorrelation makes prediction fundamentally easier than for returns. Frank (2023) extended TFT to weekly and monthly realized volatility of 355 S&P 500 stocks and found it significantly outperformed LSTM, Random Forest, and GARCH—but only with sectoral pooling across multiple stocks. Without pooling, advantages were marginal.

The broader evidence on transformers for time series is contentious. Zeng et al.'s "Are Transformers Effective for Time Series Forecasting?" (AAAI 2023, oral presentation) showed that an **embarrassingly simple one-layer linear model outperformed Informer, Autoformer, FEDformer, and other sophisticated transformer architectures** across nine benchmarks. Their key experiment demonstrated that randomly shuffling input sequences barely affected transformer performance—proof that the models were not effectively learning temporal order. PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023) partially rehabilitated transformers by using patch-based processing, but the debate remains unresolved.

For tabular data specifically—which is what cross-sectional equity features are—Grinsztajn, Oyallon, and Varoquaux (NeurIPS 2022) showed that **tree-based models remain state-of-the-art on medium-sized datasets** even without accounting for their superior speed. Their key insight: neural networks are biased toward smooth solutions, while financial data often exhibits irregular, non-smooth patterns. Krauss, Do, and Huck (2017) provided the most direct evidence for equity returns, finding that on the S&P 500, random forests generated **0.43% daily returns** versus deep learning's **0.33%**—trees beat deep learning outright. Nearly all published TFT stock market papers predict price levels rather than returns, a fundamental methodological weakness that inflates apparent accuracy through autocorrelation.

## Logistic regression is the baseline that refuses to die

Multiple independent studies converge on a finding that should give pause to anyone building complex models: **regularized logistic regression performs comparably to sophisticated ML methods for equity return classification**. Wolff (Journal of Forecasting, 2024) analyzed S&P 500 constituents from 1999–2021 and found "more simplistic regularized logistic regression models to perform similarly well compared to gradient boosting, RF, DNN, and LSTM NN." A 2025 study in Springer Nature on fundamental features showed logistic regression outperformed all ML approaches in trend-based classification with "very high t-statistics and extremely small p-values." The BenchStock benchmark (ICLR 2025 submission) reinforced this by showing prediction accuracy was not correlated with portfolio return, and advanced deep learning did not outperform traditional approaches.

Logistic regression's secret weapon is **natural calibration**. Niculescu-Mizil and Caruana's landmark ICML 2005 study, "Predicting Good Probabilities with Supervised Learning," tested 10 classifiers across 2,000 model variants and found logistic regression produces well-calibrated probabilities by construction—a consequence of its canonical logit link function. KNN, by contrast, was described as "remarkably unaffected by calibration, but exhibit[ing] mediocre overall performance." After post-hoc calibration via Platt scaling or isotonic regression, boosted trees surpassed all other methods, but uncalibrated logistic regression remained the most reliable probability estimator. KNN probabilities are inherently coarse-grained, limited to multiples of 1/k, which fundamentally constrains calibration resolution. **No published paper was found measuring Brier Score or Brier Skill Score for KNN specifically in financial prediction contexts**—a genuine gap in the literature.

## KNN earns its keep in turbulent markets and diverse ensembles

The strongest case for KNN in finance comes not from standalone prediction but from two specific applications: regime-conditional forecasting and ensemble diversity. A 2016 study in the International Journal of Forecasting, "Combining Nearest Neighbor Predictions and Model-Based Predictions of Realized Variance," examined S&P 100 stocks over 16 years and found that **in turbulent markets, nearest-neighbor forecasts led to higher risk-adjusted profitability than parametric models, even when statistically inferior overall**. The authors concluded that combining non-parametric and parametric forecasts "provides protection against model misspecification and parameter estimation uncertainty, given the distinct way in which each exploits the information set."

This finding aligns with KNN's theoretical advantage: it makes no functional-form assumptions about the data-generating process. Guégan and Huck's foundational paper "On the Use of Nearest Neighbors in Finance" (2005) articulated the appeal: "pieces of time series, in the past, might have a resemblance to pieces in the future." But they also issued a critical warning—the complexity of financial data forces practitioners to use large values of K, and standard methods for selecting embedding dimension and K "lead to distort the basic idea behind the Nearest Neighbors method." Fernández-Rodríguez, Sosvilla-Rivero, and Andrada-Félix (International Journal of Forecasting, 1999) showed nearest-neighbor predictors marginally beat random walk and ARIMA for EMS exchange rates, establishing a baseline of modest but real predictive value.

Ensemble evidence supports including KNN as a base learner alongside parametric models. An improved stacking framework for S&P 500, Dow 30, and Nasdaq prediction (~2020, Physica A) used tree-based and deep learning base learners with logistic regression as meta-classifier and outperformed all individual methods. A 2025 study in the Journal of Big Data found hybrid ensembles combining logistic regression, KNN, SVM, and Random Forest outperformed all single models, with KNN contributing diversity—"lower variance than Decision Trees, less bias than Logistic Regression." However, **no study was found that isolates KNN's marginal contribution through ablation analysis** in a financial ensemble. The theoretical argument for diversity is sound, but the empirical evidence is inferred rather than directly demonstrated.

## High dimensions hollow out KNN's core mechanism

The curse of dimensionality is not merely a theoretical concern for KNN in finance—it is the primary practical limitation. For n = 1,000 training points in d dimensions, the edge length of the hypercube containing the k = 10 nearest neighbors scales as ℓ ≈ (k/n)^(1/d). At d = 10, this requires searching **63%** of the feature space; at d = 100, it reaches **95%**. Modern equity prediction uses 50–900+ features (Gu-Kelly-Xiu used 94 characteristics with macroeconomic interactions yielding ~900 predictors), dimensions where pairwise distances concentrate and nearest versus farthest neighbors become nearly equidistant. Pestov (Computers & Mathematics with Applications, 2013) proved that k-approximate nearest neighbor classifiers are consistent in high dimensions but "provably unstable."

Practitioners address this through aggressive dimensionality reduction. Zhong and Enke (Expert Systems with Applications, 2017) used 60 financial features with PCA, fuzzy robust PCA, and kernel PCA to predict S&P 500 ETF direction, finding that dimensionality reduction significantly improved both accuracy and risk-adjusted profits. Chen and Hao (Expert Systems with Applications, 2017) proposed Feature Weighted KNN (FWKNN), using information gain to weight features in distance calculations rather than treating all equally. A practitioner implementation on TradingView reduces features to just **three principal components** (momentum, mean reversion, dynamics) before applying KNN—suggesting experienced traders consider 3–5 effective dimensions optimal. The comprehensive survey by Ghimire et al. (Financial Innovation, 2023) reviewed 32+ papers and found PCA is the most popular technique, with the consensus that dimensionality reduction consistently improves ML prediction performance, and KNN benefits the most due to its distance-based nature.

Approximate nearest neighbor methods like HNSW (Malkov and Yashunin, IEEE TPAMI, 2018) and FAISS (Douze et al., 2024) solve the computational scaling problem but not the statistical one. HNSW achieves **0.9–0.99 recall** while being orders of magnitude faster than brute-force search—relevant when searching millions of historical patterns. KX (the kdb+ vendor) explicitly markets temporal similarity search for Wall Street applications, citing quant research, execution analytics, and algorithmic trading as use cases. **However, no peer-reviewed paper benchmarks HNSW versus exact KNN specifically for financial analogue matching.** The gap between practitioner adoption and academic validation is wide. Approximate methods help with speed but do not address the fundamental problem that in high-dimensional noisy financial data, the "nearest" neighbors may not be meaningfully similar.

## Conclusion: when to use KNN and when to reach for something else

The evidence assembled here supports several clear conclusions and several honest uncertainties. **Gradient boosted trees (XGBoost, LightGBM) dominate KNN for cross-sectional equity return prediction** across every published comparison, and KNN's absence from top-tier asset pricing benchmarks reflects justified skepticism. Logistic regression remains a surprisingly robust baseline that matches complex models in multiple independent studies and offers superior probability calibration by construction. Transformers and TFT have not demonstrated reliable improvement over tree-based methods for return prediction specifically, though TFT shows genuine promise for volatility forecasting with rich feature sets.

KNN's defensible niche is narrower than its intuitive appeal suggests. It contributes meaningfully as a **diversity source in stacked ensembles**, where its non-parametric error pattern complements parametric models. It shows evidence of **regime-conditional economic value in turbulent markets**, where parametric model assumptions break down. And it may excel in **very low-dimensional settings** (3–15 effective features after PCA) for specific pattern-matching tasks. But the direct question—does analogue matching outperform regression on the same features?—has no convincing affirmative answer in the published literature. The evidence that exists (Guégan and Huck, 2005; Fernández-Rodríguez et al., 1999) shows marginal advantages over random walks in specific markets, not superiority over modern parametric alternatives. The honest summary is that KNN is a useful tool for specific, well-constrained applications in quantitative finance, but it is not a competitive standalone method for the broad equity return prediction problem that gradient boosted trees now own.
